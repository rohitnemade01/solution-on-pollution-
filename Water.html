What is water pollution?

Water pollution occurs when harmful substances—often chemicals or microorganisms—contaminate a stream, river, lake, ocean, aquifer, or other body of water, degrading water quality and rendering it toxic to humans or the environment.

This widespread problem of water pollution is jeopardizing our health. Unsafe water kills more people each year than war and all other forms of violence combined. Meanwhile, our drinkable water sources are finite: Less than 1 percent of the earth’s freshwater is actually accessible to us. Without action, the challenges will only increase by 2050, when global demand for freshwater is expected to be one-third greater than it is now.

What are the causes of water pollution?

Water is uniquely vulnerable to pollution. Known as a “universal solvent,” water is able to dissolve more substances than any other liquid on earth. It’s the reason we have Kool-Aid and brilliant blue waterfalls. It’s also why water is so easily polluted. Toxic substances from farms, towns, and factories readily dissolve into and mix with it, causing water pollution.

What are the effects of water pollution?

On human health

Water pollution has severe effects on human health, as it exposes individuals to harmful contaminants, toxins, and microorganisms. Contaminated water often contains pathogens that can cause diseases such as cholera, typhoid, and dysentery, leading to widespread illness and even fatalities, particularly in areas with inadequate sanitation. Heavy metals like lead and mercury, commonly found in polluted water, can cause long-term health problems, including kidney damage, neurological disorders, and developmental issues in children. Consumption of water polluted with industrial chemicals or agricultural runoffs, such as pesticides and fertilizers, may result in cancer, hormonal imbalances, and reproductive issues. Additionally, polluted water used for bathing or cooking can lead to skin infections and gastrointestinal illnesses, further impacting quality of life and increasing healthcare burdens.

On the environment

Water pollution significantly disrupts the environment by degrading aquatic ecosystems and harming biodiversity. Pollutants like chemicals, plastics, and heavy metals contaminate rivers, lakes, and oceans, making them uninhabitable for many species. Toxic substances can accumulate in the food chain, affecting not only aquatic organisms but also terrestrial animals and humans who rely on them. Excess nutrients from agricultural runoff lead to eutrophication, which depletes oxygen levels in water, causing the death of fish and other marine life. Oil spills coat marine animals and plants, disrupting their natural processes and leading to long-term ecological damage. Polluted water bodies lose their natural ability to purify themselves, affecting their role in sustaining ecosystems and regulating climate. Overall, water pollution destabilizes ecosystems, diminishes biodiversity, and hinders the natural balance of life on Earth.
